From Single Prompts to Small Workflows
Week 3 · Logistics Disruption Response

The same event needs to be explained twice — to operations, and to the customer. In two different tones.

Real organisational tasks are almost never single-step. A logistics disruption isn't "tell the customer" — it's "explain to ops what to do, then tell the customer something appropriate, in a different tone, without compromising the operations message." This week you'll build your first two-stage workflow: one input, two parallel outputs, two audiences.

For Operations
Direct, action-oriented. What to do, by when, who owns it.
For the Customer
Empathetic, reassuring. What this means for them, and how it's being fixed.
1The Trap

What happens when you try to do this in a single prompt.

Below is a real disruption event landing in your inbox at 6:47 AM. A naive approach is to send one prompt: "draft a reply." Click the button and see what falls out — and what's missing.

Incoming Disruption · 06:47 IST
Truck breakdown on NH48 — Mumbai-Pune corridor
Vehicle MH-12-PD-4421 carrying 312 outbound packages has suffered a transmission failure 47 km from Pune hub. Recovery vehicle dispatched. Estimated repair + transit time: 18 hours. 187 customers affected. Delivery slots booked for today between 11 AM and 8 PM.
Packages: 312
Affected customers: 187
Delay: ~18 hrs
SLA breach: YES
→ You: "Given this logistics disruption, draft a reply to send out."

Dear Customer,

We regret to inform you that due to an unforeseen vehicular issue on the Mumbai-Pune highway, your delivery may be delayed by approximately 18 hours. Our team is working hard to resolve this. We apologise for the inconvenience caused.

Please bear with us. Our operations team has been alerted and a recovery vehicle has been dispatched. Affected customers will receive their packages tomorrow. Thank you for your patience.

— Customer Care Team

What's wrong?  The output mixes two purposes badly. Operations receives no actionable detail — no who-does-what, no SLA-breach handling, no escalation flag. The customer gets internal phrases like "recovery vehicle dispatched" that mean nothing to them. One audience would have been better served if the other hadn't existed. That's the case for splitting the work into a workflow.

5Three Ways to Build It

No code. Three honest options. Pick the one that fits your stack.

The exact same workflow can be assembled in three different no-code environments. The mechanics are nearly identical; the difference is what's around it — your existing tools, your IT/security policies, your team's comfort. Pick one and own its trade-offs honestly.

G
OpenAI · Free w/ Plus

Two linked Custom GPTs

One GPT analyses the event, then routes structured output to two specialist GPTs — one for ops, one for customer messaging. Cheapest, fastest setup. Lives inside ChatGPT.

6The Vocabulary

Two definitions to take with you.

These are the terms you'll use the rest of the year. Knowing them precisely lets you describe what you're building — and recognise it when a vendor tries to sell you something different.

Definition

Workflow (in the AI sense)

A chain of two or more AI steps where the output of one step becomes the input of the next, possibly with branching or human checkpoints in the middle. Workflows are how single-shot prompts grow up into systems.

Definition

Tone Separation

The discipline of producing audience-appropriate output even when both audiences are responding to the same event. The internal operations team wants direct, action-oriented language. The affected customer wants empathy, clarity, and reassurance. The same factual situation, expressed in two registers.

7An Honest Reflection

What this workflow can't do — and shouldn't try.

Demos make workflows look magical. Production makes them look mortal. Before you ship one, hold these limits clearly in mind. None of them is a reason not to build the workflow — but each one is a reason to think about where the human stays in the loop.

!
It can't read context outside the prompt

If the disruption involves a VIP client, a regulatory threshold, or a recent escalation history — and that context isn't fed in — the workflow will produce a generic, technically correct, contextually tone-deaf message.

?
It can't tell when to escalate to a human

The system has no instinct for "this one is different." A passenger death, a viral social media moment, a regulator's tweet — these need a person, not a workflow. Build an explicit escalation rule, don't assume the AI will sense it.

≈
Tone separation can drift over time

As you tune the customer message to be warmer, the ops message can creep towards the same register. Periodically read both side-by-side and ask: would my warehouse manager and my customer both feel served? If not, retune.

⌖
Multi-audience nuance still requires a human

A B2B customer and a B2C customer should not receive the same email, even if both are affected by the same delay. A two-output workflow handles two audiences — not four. Decide consciously how many branches you need.
