Module 04 · Small Workflows

From single prompts to small workflows

Real work is never one step. A delayed shipment isn't "write a reply" — it's understand, decide, brief the team, write the customer. This week you'll build your first two-stage workflow: one input, two parallel outputs, two different tones, no code.

★ The core insight
When your boss says "handle it" they mean five things at once. Investigate, decide, brief, communicate, log. A workflow is just naming those steps out loud — and giving each one its own room to think.

Definition
Workflow (in the AI sense)

A chain of two or more AI steps where the output of one step becomes the input of the next, possibly with branching or human checkpoints in the middle. Workflows are how single-shot prompts grow up into systems.

Tone separation

The discipline of producing audience-appropriate output even when both audiences are responding to the same event. The internal operations team wants direct, action-oriented language. Lab 5 · From Concept to Tool

Build this in thirty minutes, three ways

Three no-code paths to the same outcome. Pick the one that fits where your work already lives. None of them ask you to write a line of code.

G

Two linked Custom GPTs

Best for · Conversational chains

Build two custom GPTs (or Claude Projects). The first analyses. The second pair writes the outputs. You "hand off" by pasting the analysis between them.

  1. In ChatGPT or Claude, create "Incident Analyser" — give it the instruction: "Extract what happened, severity 1-5, audience, action needed"
  2. Create "Tone-Split Writer" — instruction: "Given an incident analysis, produce both an ops brief and a customer message"
  3. To run: paste situation into Analyser, copy its output, paste into the Writer
  4. Save both as starter prompts inside a shared workspace so the team can reuse them
Why pick it: Fastest path. No subscriptions. Easiest to refine — change the instruction in plain English and re-run.
Z

Zapier or Make.com

Best for · True automation

A scenario that triggers on a form submission or email, runs the analyser, then splits into two parallel paths — each calls an AI step and writes to a different destination (Slack, email, ticket).

  1. Create a new Zap or Make scenario. Trigger: "New form submission" or "Email received with subject INCIDENT"
  2. Add an AI step (OpenAI/Anthropic action) configured as the Analyser. Map its output to a variable
  3. Add two parallel paths: one AI step writes the ops brief (posts to #incidents); the other writes the customer message (drafts in Gmail)
  4. Run a test event. Check both outputs land in the right places, in the right tone
Why pick it: Runs without you. Great when the trigger is automatic (form, alert, monitoring system) and you want both outputs delivered without copy-paste.
M

Copilot Studio

Best for · M365 estates

Build the workflow as a Copilot agent with multiple actions and audience-aware variables. Lives inside Teams and respects your tenancy's data boundaries.

  1. In Copilot Studio, create a new agent. Add a "topic" triggered by an incident phrase
  2. Configure the first action: "Generate an incident analysis using the input situation"
  3. Add two follow-up actions referencing the analysis variable — one for ops tone, one for customer tone
  4. Publish to your Teams channel. Tag the agent in #incidents and it'll respond with both outputs
Why pick it: Compliance-friendly. Data stays in your Microsoft tenancy. The right choice when InfoSec needs a signed paper trail.
Lab 6 · Did it land?

Three questions to own this idea

No marks. Just three reality checks before you propose this workflow to your team and someone says "couldn't we just do this with one prompt?"

Q1. Your colleague says: "Why not just write a longer prompt — 'analyse this situation, then write an ops brief and a customer message'? Why bother chaining?"
Q2. You build the workflow in Zapier. It runs on form submissions and sends customer emails automatically. After two weeks, a customer receives a message that misreads the situation badly. What's the right structural fix?
Q3. Your org runs on Microsoft 365 and InfoSec is nervous about data leaving the tenancy. Marketing wants this workflow yesterday. Which tool do you propose?
🔗

A workflow is a diagram, not a prompt

Once you draw it on paper — boxes, arrows, who reads what — the rest is just configuration. The thinking happens in the design phase.

🎭

One source of truth, two tones

The internal team and the customer are reading about the same event. They need different words. A branching workflow gives you that for free.

🛑

Human checkpoints are a feature

The point of a workflow isn't full automation. It's to make each step inspectable. The customer-facing branch almost always deserves a human glance before it sends.
