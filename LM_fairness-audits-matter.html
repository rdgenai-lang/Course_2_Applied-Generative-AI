Module 05 · Fairness Audits

Why fairness audits matter in AI work

There is a specific moment when you find out whether your AI system treats different groups equally. It is rarely a moment you choose — usually a journalist, a regulator, or a furious customer notices first. The reliable way to avoid that discovery is to look for it yourself, before deployment.

★ The Core Insight
An AI system is going to tell you whether it's fair. The only question is whether you'll be the one asking — or whether you'll find out from the regulator, the journalist, or the class-action lawyer who got there first.
Lab 1 · The Discovery Moment

Three companies that found out the hard way

Patterns drawn from real, well-documented cases. Each one had a moment when the bias became visible. None of these companies chose that moment.

Hiring · 2018

The CV screener that learned to reject women

A large tech firm built an AI to score CVs from a decade of past hires.

How it surfaced
An internal team noticed the tool was downgrading CVs that included the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club captain" or "women's college". The training data reflected a decade of male-dominated hiring; the AI learned to perpetuate it.
Tool scrapped
Years of engineering wasted
Brand impact
Lending · 2019

The credit card with mysteriously different limits

A major card issuer launched an AI underwriting system. Couples filing jointly noticed something odd.

How it surfaced
A husband and wife with identical finances applied. He got a credit limit ten times higher than hers. He tweeted about it. By morning the story was global. The issuer's response — "the algorithm is gender-blind" — turned out to be true and also irrelevant to the harm.
Regulator opened probe
CEO Senate testimony
Reputational damage
Healthcare · 2019

The risk score that ignored the sickest

A widely-deployed clinical algorithm used cost-of-care as a proxy for medical need.

How it surfaced
Researchers audited the tool and found that at any given risk score, Black patients were significantly sicker than white patients. The cause: historical under-spending on Black patients was being treated by the algorithm as "lower need." A proxy was being mistaken for the truth.
Affected ~100M patients
Published in Science
Industry-wide reform
The pattern is consistent: the bias was real, the system was technically working as designed, and the cost of late discovery — measured in trust, regulation, and engineering re-work — was enormous compared to the cost of auditing before deployment.
Lab 2 · Demographic Parity

Now you'll audit a system yourself

An AI tool has scored 200 candidates for an interview. Each gets a "fit score" 0-100. Anyone above your chosen threshold gets an interview. Your job is to ask the first audit question: do different groups get interviewed at the same rate?

Definition
Demographic parity

A fairness criterion that asks: across different groups (gender, age bracket, region, language), does the system produce favourable outcomes at roughly equal rates? It is not the only fairness criterion, and it is not appropriate for every situation, but it is the one most often referred to.

★ The Subtler Question
Demographic parity asks "are outcomes equal?" — but a deeper audit asks a different question. Are mistakes equal? Because two groups can be selected at the same rate while the system makes very different mistakes about each.
Lab 3 · Equalised Odds

Now reveal who was actually qualified

In any real audit, ground truth eventually arrives — through hiring outcomes, loan repayments, medical results. When we line up the AI's decisions against reality, a new picture appears. Some "rejections" weren't just unfair — they were wrong.

Definition
Equalised odds

A fairness criterion that asks: across different groups, are the system's false-positive and false-negative rates roughly equal? Where demographic parity asks "are outcomes equal," equalised odds asks "are mistakes equal" — a subtler and often more useful question.

Where the harm lives
At threshold 60, Group B is more than 2x as likely to be wrongly rejected as Group A. The system isn't just unbalanced — it's making systematically more mistakes about one group's qualified people. This is the harm that demographic parity alone might never reveal.
Definition
Disparate impact

A legal and regulatory concept used in many jurisdictions to identify when a system produces materially worse outcomes for a protected group, regardless of whether it was designed to. It is the framing a regulator will most likely use when challenging an AI deployment in hiring, lending, or insurance. Intent is irrelevant; outcome is everything.

Lab 5 · From Idea to Tool

Three ways to actually run a fairness audit

None of them require Python. They scale from a single spreadsheet to a regulated enterprise dashboard to a paid third-party audit. Pick the one that fits where you are.

S

The Spreadsheet Audit

Best for · One-off checks

Excel or Google Sheets. Rows are test cases. Columns are the protected attribute, the AI score, the actual outcome (when known). Built-in formulas give you parity ratio and false-negative rate per group.

  1. Create columns: case_id, group, ai_score, ai_decision, ground_truth (if available)
  2. Add a SELECTED column: =IF(ai_score >= threshold, "yes", "no") with threshold in a named cell
  3. Use COUNTIFS to count selected-by-group; divide to get selection rate; smaller÷larger gives parity ratio
  4. For equalised odds: COUNTIFS where ground_truth=qualified AND selected=no → divide by total qualified per group = FNR
Why pick it: Auditable by anyone in the org. No vendor required. Becomes evidence in itself — the spreadsheet IS the audit trail.
R

Responsible AI Dashboard

Best for · Production systems

Microsoft's Responsible AI Dashboard (open-source · GUI) or IBM's AI Fairness 360 toolkit. Upload your predictions and ground-truth labels; the tool slices the metrics across protected attributes and produces audit-ready charts.

  1. Install the dashboard (one-click in Azure ML Studio or as a Jupyter widget — no model code required)
  2. Upload your prediction file: model output + protected attribute + ground truth in three columns
  3. Select fairness metrics: demographic parity difference, equalised odds, equal opportunity
  4. Export the report as PDF or share the interactive dashboard link with risk & compliance
Why pick it: Visual, repeatable, and the output is what regulators have begun to expect to see. Lives in the same stack as your model deployment.
A

Audit-as-a-Service

Best for · Regulated deployments

Specialised firms (Holistic AI, Credo AI, ORCAA, BABL AI) provide third-party audits with sign-off. Increasingly required in EU AI Act, NYC Local Law 144, and emerging regulations across India, Singapore, and the UK.

  1. Identify which jurisdiction(s) your AI is deployed in — and which classify your use case as "high-risk"
  2. Engage a vendor early: most audits are pre-deployment, not post-mortem. The audit shapes the system
  3. Provide model documentation, training data summaries, and a hold-out test set with protected attributes
  4. Receive a signed audit report. In some jurisdictions you must publish a summary — read it carefully before you do
Why pick it: Independent voice. Legal defensibility. When the inevitable question "did you audit this?" comes, the answer is a stamped document.
Lab 6 · Did it land?

Three questions to own this idea

No marks. Just three reality checks before you next sit in a meeting where someone says "we don't need to audit it — it's not a discriminatory system."

Q1. Your hiring AI gets adjusted so that interview rates match across men and women (45% each). A junior analyst then audits the false negative rate and finds it's 12% for men and 30% for women. The team says "we passed demographic parity — we're done." Are they done?
Q2. You discover a deployed AI system shows disparate impact in production. The fastest fix is to disable the tool entirely. The slow fix is to retrain on better data over three months. The pragmatic middle option is to keep the tool but add a human reviewer for borderline cases. What should you do FIRST?
Q3. Your team audited the system once during build. The model hasn't changed since. Six months in, do you re-audit?
🔍

Audit before the journalist does

The moment of bias-discovery isn't optional. The only choice is whether you'll be the one finding it, or whether you'll learn about it from someone you didn't pick.

⚖️

One fairness metric is never enough

A system can pass demographic parity and fail equalised odds (or vice versa). Real audits look at both — and report honestly when they disagree.

📋

The discipline is the deliverable

You don't need Python or a vendor to audit. You need three things: a definition of harm, test cases that exercise it, and the willingness to document what you find.
