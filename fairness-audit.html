Why Fairness Audits Matter
Week 4 · Auditing an AI Resume Screener

There's a specific moment you discover your AI is unfair. Usually somebody else notices first.

A journalist. A regulator. A class-action lawyer. A furious customer. The reliable way to avoid that discovery is to look for it yourself, deliberately and structurally, before deployment. This week — using a real AI hiring system as our laboratory — you'll do exactly that.

4Disparate Impact

The 4/5ths rule — the metric regulators actually use.

When a regulator challenges your AI in hiring, lending, or insurance, they probably won't use the words "demographic parity." 7The Vocabulary

Three terms regulators, lawyers, and journalists already use.

You'll meet these in vendor pitches, in regulator notices, and in court filings. Knowing them precisely lets you tell the difference between a thoughtful audit claim and marketing froth.

Definition

Demographic Parity

A fairness criterion that asks: across different groups (gender, age bracket, region, language), does the system produce favourable outcomes at roughly equal rates? It is not the only fairness criterion — and not appropriate for every situation — but it is the one most often referred to.

Asks: "Are the outcomes equal?"
Definition

Equalised Odds

A fairness criterion that asks: across different groups, are the system's false-positive and false-negative rates roughly equal? Where demographic parity asks "are outcomes equal," equalised odds asks "are mistakes equal" — a subtler and often more useful question.

Asks: "Are the mistakes equal?"
Definition

Disparate Impact

A legal and regulatory concept used in many jurisdictions to identify when a system produces materially worse outcomes for a protected group, regardless of whether it was designed to. It is the framing a regulator will most likely use when challenging an AI deployment in hiring, lending, or insurance.

Asks: "Is the harm material — by law?"
Bias auditing, despite its technical reputation, is a discipline that lives in three things: a clear definition of who could be harmed, a set of test cases that exercise that risk, and the willingness to document what you find honestly.
01
Define the harm

Before any maths, name the groups your system could plausibly affect — and why.

02
Run the tests

Slice the data. Run counterfactual pairs. Compute the disparate impact ratio. Look.

03
Document honestly

Write what you found — including failures. The technical sophistication matters less than the seriousness of your intent.
