Lesson 01 · Triage & Classification

Most professional reading isn't reading.
It's sorting.

A help-desk lead skims a hundred queries. A claims handler sorts insurance submissions. A bank routes complaints. None of it is profound — but at volume it's exhausting. This is exactly where AI starts to earn its keep.

2Triage is everywhere

It's not just inboxes. Every large organisation does this.

Across industries, professionals spend hours every week reading not for insight, but to decide where something belongs and who should handle it.

Help-desk lead

Skims a hundred customer queries each morning and decides which need urgent attention.

Claims handler

Bank operations

Talent partner

3What "classification" really means

It's just putting text into buckets.

No magic. Classification is the act of taking one piece of text and dropping it into one of a few pre-defined categories. Watch the four steps below.

1
Input
A piece of text arrives — could be an email, a query, a CV.
2
Analyse
The AI reads it and looks for patterns it has seen before.
3
Decide
It picks the single best-matching bucket from the list you defined.
4
Output
A simple label — that label routes the work to the right place.
"Is this email a complaint, a question, or feedback?" · "Which of our four products does this query relate to?" · "Is this CV junior, mid-level, or senior?" — all classification problems.

📘 Definition

Few-shot classification

A pattern where you give an AI tool a handful of example input/output pairs — five to ten is usually enough — and then ask it to apply the same classification to new inputs. It works astonishingly well for tasks that would have required days of model training a decade ago.

5Few-shot in action

Watch it work, step by step.

Here's the exact recipe — what you show the AI, what arrives next, and what comes back.

1
Show 5 labelled examples

You write a short list of "input → label" pairs. That's the whole training step.

"My card was charged twice" → complaint
"How do I reset my PIN?" → question
"Love the new app design" → feedback
"Why is your IVR so slow?" → complaint
"What time does support open?" → question
2
The AI infers the pattern

"I see — angry/frustrated language tends to be complaint, anything ending in '?' or asking 'how/when/what' is a question, and praise is feedback."

3
A brand-new message arrives

Something the AI has never seen before.

"I tried to add a beneficiary in the app for thirty minutes and it just keeps timing out — this is incredibly frustrating."
4
The AI returns a clean label
✓ complaint

Routed automatically to the digital-banking complaints queue. Total time: under one second.

6The safety net · Confidence thresholds

But what if the AI isn't sure?

The single most important guardrail in any automated decision system — and the one that almost never appears in the demo.

AI's confidence on each query
"My card was double-charged for the same hotel booking."
96% · auto-route
"What's the daily ATM limit for premium accounts?"
88% · auto-route
Threshold: 85%
"Hi I want to know about the thing my husband set up last year — the new one."
52% · human review

If the AI is less than 85% sure, a human looks at it.

That's the entire safety idea. Most queries are clear and the AI handles them in milliseconds. The unclear ones — vague, garbled, multi-topic, emotional — get routed to a person who can ask follow-ups.

  • High-confidence cases auto-resolve, freeing human attention.
  • Low-confidence cases get human judgment, where it actually matters.
  • You get speed where it's safe — and care where it's needed.
📘 Definition

Confidence threshold

A rule that says "if the system is less than X percent sure of its answer, route it to a human." A confidence threshold is the most important guardrail in any automated decision system, and it almost never appears in the demo. It always appears in the production version.
